Don't post this

✕
Calendar and holiday content
Happy Easter. Happy March Break. Happy Back to School. Every local tutoring company posts this — which is exactly why we don't. It fills the feed with content that has nothing to do with why parents follow us, and it signals we've run out of things to say. The Easter tulips post is the example. When in doubt, ask: could a physiotherapy clinic post this? If yes, don't.
✕
Stock-photo-style children
Smiling kid with a book. Backpack. Apple on a desk. It looks like every other education brand and undermines the editorial, clinical credibility in every other post. If an image could be on a competitor's page, it doesn't belong on ours.
✕
Vague inspiration with no edge
"Every child can learn to read!" True. Harmless. Forgettable. No information, no point of view, no reason to follow us. If a post could have been written by anyone, it wasn't written by Lark.
✕
Reposting without adding Lark's voice
Sharing someone else's content with no commentary signals that we don't have anything of our own to say. If we reshare something, Lark's one-sentence reaction — the insider's take — is the post. The reshare is just the evidence.
✕
Softening the brand to avoid offending teachers or the board
Lark is inside the system and calling it out. That tension is the whole brand. Posts that hedge, qualify, and reassure everyone that school is actually great just cancel the thing that makes Radical Reading worth following. We respect teachers. We do not protect the system.
✕
Manufactured urgency or false scarcity
"Only 2 spots left!" when there are 14. Parents notice, and it destroys the one thing we're building — trust. If spots are genuinely low, say so with the real number. Honest urgency only.

How the algorithm ranks your posts

Engagement signals — in order of importance
Tap to expand
1
Saves — the highest-value action on any post
When someone saves a post, Instagram reads it as "this was worth keeping." Saves trigger wider distribution more reliably than anything else. Design educational posts to be save-worthy: checklists, explainers, reference content parents return to.
2
Shares — "this was worth sending to someone"
Shares to Stories and DMs are the second-highest signal. The most share-worthy Radical Reading posts are ones that make a parent think "I need to send this to another parent." The Lexia/TDSB content is a natural share trigger.
3
Comments — especially in the first 60 minutes
Comments signal active conversation. A specific question in the caption invites real replies. Lark replies to every comment within 30 minutes of posting.
4
Time spent on post
Carousel posts naturally increase time-on-post because people swipe through. This is why carousels outperform single images for educational content.
5
Likes — low signal, mostly ignore
Nearly worthless as an algorithmic signal now. Don't optimise for them and don't be discouraged by a post with few likes but strong saves.

Instagram first shows your post to a small test group — roughly your most engaged existing followers. If that group engages well, distribution expands. This is why the first 60 minutes after posting matters, and why posting when your audience is active is critical.

Post formats — what to use and when

⊞
Carousel Use now
2–10 images, swipe-through. The save magnet. Use for: track explainer, summer slide, Matthew Effect, parent FAQ, any educational content with multiple points. First slide must stop the scroll — treat it like a headline.
▣
Single image Use now
Best for Truth Bombs — one sharp message, one Red Pen graphic. Also for the Summer Surge announcement and conversion posts where one image says everything.
◎
Stories David now / Lark later
Disappear in 24 hours — lowest brand risk on the platform. David posts: reshares of feed posts, "link in bio" reminders, Summer Surge countdown. Lark's on-ramp when ready: a text-slide Story, no camera required. Replies to Stories are highly weighted by the algorithm.
▷
Reels Not yet
Highest algorithmic reach of any format. Requires video — a comfort barrier for Lark right now. Revisit in the fall when there's traction. A 30-second iPhone clip in good window light is enough when the time comes.

The first 60 minutes — engagement seeding

1
Post at the right time
Toronto parent demographic: Tuesday and Thursday 8–9am, Sunday 7–8pm. Confirm against your own Insights once you have 30+ days of data — your specific audience may differ slightly.
2
Ask a specific question in every caption
Not "what do you think?" — something answerable. "Did your child's school ever mention Lexia to you?" or "Which track sounds like your kid — IGNITE, SURGE, or BRIDGE?" Specific questions get specific replies.
3
Reply to every comment within 30 minutes
Even a one-word reply counts. It signals to the algorithm the post is generating conversation and triggers another round of distribution. David can cover this if Lark isn't available at post time.
4
Engage with 2–3 other accounts right after posting
Log in as the brand account and leave a genuine comment on a followed account's recent post. Being active on the platform immediately after posting signals the account is live.
5
David can seed from a personal account
A genuine comment from a personal account in the first 15 minutes helps trigger the test-group expansion. Not "great post!" — an actual reaction.

Instagram Insights — what to track

Track — monthly (account level)
Follower count Note on the last Sunday of each month.
Accounts reached Unique accounts who saw anything from you.
Profile visits How many people checked you out. The vetting signal.
Website taps Clicks on your bio link. Your conversion signal.
Follower location Confirm East Toronto parents are your actual audience.
Track — per post (at 48 hours)
Saves Most important single metric. Track obsessively.
Shares Second most important.
Comments Third.
Follows from post Did this post bring in new followers?
Reach Unique accounts who saw this post.
Don't bother tracking
Likes Too low-signal to be meaningful.
Raw impressions Only meaningful relative to reach — alone it tells you nothing.
The one number to watch
Profile visits ÷ website taps
Your bio conversion rate. 50 visits, 3 taps = 6%. Lower means the profile itself needs work, not the posts.
Monthly check-in ritual — 15 minutes
Last Sunday of each month. Open Insights, note follower count, reached accounts, profile visits, website taps. Look at the top 3 posts by saves. Ask: what did those posts have in common? Do more of that.
LinkedIn is a different job than Instagram

Instagram talks directly to parents who might enroll. LinkedIn reaches educators, SLPs, and dyslexia advocates who refer families — and journalists and policy people who can amplify Lark's voice. One well-placed LinkedIn post reaches an audience Instagram never will. When those professionals share it, it lands in exactly the right parent communities.

What to post — and what not to

✓
Insider educator perspective
Things Lark sees inside the system that parents and policy people don't. The Lexia fidelity problem. The gap between the Right to Read recommendations and what's actually happening in classrooms. This is her unique voice — use it.
✓
Blog posts — with native commentary, not just a link
LinkedIn deprioritises posts that are only a link. Always lead with 2–3 sentences of Lark's original commentary before the URL. The post intro below is ready to use for the first blog share.
✓
Right to Read and Ontario literacy policy updates
Lark is embedded in this movement. When there are developments, she's one of the first people who should be commenting publicly. Keeps her visible in the policy and advocate layer her network cares about.
✓
Honest Radical Reading milestones
When Summer Surge fills. When the first families complete the program. When there's a real outcome to share. Not promotional — just honest professional updates from someone building something meaningful.
✕
Product promotions or enrollment CTAs
LinkedIn's educator and professional audience disengages immediately from anything that reads like an ad. The referral value comes from Lark being seen as a credible thought leader. Radical Reading mentions should always feel organic, never salesy.

Cadence

Once a week is ideal. Once every two weeks is fine. Substance over volume — a single well-written post from a credentialed educator lands far better than frequent lightweight content. LinkedIn rewards depth in a way Instagram doesn't.
